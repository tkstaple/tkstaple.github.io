Foodfully

An app to eliminate household food waste. Save money, waste less, eat better.


[ foodful.ly ]
  • Branding
  • Research
2015—present

Purpose

To design an app to reduce and eliminate household food waste. Attempt to change people's understanding about food waste and curb wastage.

Context

Role: Lead Designer
Food waste in the United States remains an an important economic, social and environmental issue. The average American household throws away roughly $2,000 worth of food each year resulting in over 74 billion pounds of wasted food. This amounts annual losses of over $165 billion—slightly greater than the 2015 federal budget allocated for veterans’ benefits.

The issue of food waste also raises difficult questions related to social ethics. While American’s throw away roughly one-third of their food, over 46 million of their fellow citizens are collecting food stamps. Moreover, all of the work that went into growing the food is wasted as well—the labor of the farmer, the oil needed to ship the food, and the water used to grow it (from the Foodfully report: Defining Household Food Waste).

Chapter 1: Branding

Logo

My very first task when joining the company was to rebrand. Below is the evolution of the logo highlighting its most definitive changes.

Original logo (left) designed by Jack Leng; middle logo designed jointly with Samantha Spargo.

We wanted to elicit feelings of freshness and flavorfulness through the brand, since one of the goals of the app is to eat food as far away from the spoilage date as possible; thus, it was apparent that the logo should be produce of some kind, rather than something purely typographical.

Because one of the most unique and significant functions of the app is to notify users of food that will spoil soon, I narrowed down all vegetables and fruit that looked similar to an exclamation point, since it has strong (western) associations with notifications or alerts. After testing colors and forms through sketching, I finally decided on *drumroll* the radish.

Original sketches of final logo

Also, due to the dual-structure of the app (more on that in Ch.2), I stylized the logo in a split-shade style. This style is repeated other aspects of the brand identity, including the pattern, badges, and other graphic elements.

Pattern

The pattern successfully elicits associations of freshness through the variety of produce and their vibrant colors. The pattern is also very flexible in regards to online and print content.

Badges

Badges is just one way the Foodfully app is gamified in order to change users' food waste habits. The badges that are dished out (pun intended) are proportional to the recommended amount of the five USDA MyPlate food groups.

Chapter 2: The App

My biggest task was to design the Foodfully app itself. No app exists that tackles the issue of food waste in this way, so it was imperative to design a clear and intuitive experience.

Information Architecture

The two main functions of the app are to 1) show and notify users of soon-to-be-spoiled food and 2) to suggest recipes so users know how to cook the food before it goes bad. The app is divided into the Fridge and Stove sections to reflect these two distinct functions.

The Fridge shows purchased items as well as the calculated spoilage date, whereas the Stove provides recipe suggestions which use the food in the Fridge. The recipes are organized hierarchically in regards to how many items the recipe uses, so the user can use the most items before spoilage as possible.

Behavior Modification

Again, the main goal of the app is to "nudge" users to waste less food; alongside gamification, reminders are the most significant tool in doing so.

This follows BJ Fogg's behavioral model for Persuasive Design of Motivation, Ability, and Triggers. The recipes provide the Ability for users to reduce food waste, while notifications act as the Triggers. One of the main reasons people throw away food is not knowing what to do with it; hence, it is vital for notifications to provide recipe suggestions—to increase the Ability as much as possible.

Chapter 3: Everything Else

Being the lead designer for a startup means designing not only the product, but . . . everything else: website(s), pitch decks, business cards, videos, posters, and more. It is a lot of work, but I find the variety exciting.

Video

Our video was one of the first promotional pieces we made. The original video was shot and edited by Sam Newton Media, and stars one of Foodfully's charismatic former employees. I later added motion design to transform the old video in order to reflect changes in the app and the brand identity.

The new edits were made with Adobe AfterEffects CC.

Website

The main purpose of the website is to show visitors what the app does, what the company's goal is, and who makes up the company itself. I designed the website jointly with fellow designer Michael Villas and coded the website in Bootstrap. We wanted the site to reflect the general template of other startups, since they are fairly easy to navigate and we wanted to establish Foodfully as a startup aesthetically.
