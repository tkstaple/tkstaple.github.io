Foodfully

Save money, waste less, eat better.


foodful.ly 2015—2017

Goal

Design an app to increase awareness of food expiration and ultimately reduce household food waste by nudging users to eat or cook the foods that expire the soonest.

Context

Role: Lead Designer

Food waste in the United States remains an an important economic, social and environmental issue. The average American household throws away roughly $2,000 worth of food each year resulting in over 74 billion pounds of wasted food. This amounts annual losses of over $165 billion—slightly greater than the 2015 federal budget allocated for veterans’ benefits.

The issue of food waste also raises difficult questions related to social ethics. While Americans throw away roughly one-third of their food, over 46 million of their fellow citizens are collecting food stamps. Moreover, all of the work that went into growing the food is wasted as well—the labor of the farmer, the oil needed to ship the food, and the water used to grow it (from the Foodfully report: Defining Household Food Waste).

User Research

Research began by asking people a simple question: How and why does your food go bad at home?

The most common answers we received were:

  1. People generally did not know the expiration dates of their food. Even so, the dates printed on the labels weren’t always so accurate (items like milk and eggs, for example)
  2. People generally forgot about the foods they purchased. Think vegetables in those refrigerator drawers.
  3. People didn’t know how to cook the foods they bought.

For those who didn’t have these issues, another related hurdle for people was meal planning, which we took into consideration when designing the app.

Information Architecture

Based on our user research, we decided to split the app (literally) in two main features to address food waste based on our discovery:

The Fridge which addressed the expiration date problem by displaying all items bought at the store ordered hierarchically by their calculated expiration dates.The Stove which addressed the cooking problem by providing recipe suggestions based on the food items in the Fridge.

Behavioral Design

In order to nudge users to cook their soon-to-be-spoiled food via the Stove, we aimed to provide timely notifications with delicious recipes—prioritizing the foods that would expire the soonest, so users would never have to worry about forgetting.

This notification pattern follows BJ Fogg's Behavior Model. Motivation was the desire to save money and reduce food waste, Prompts were the notifications of foods about to spoil and subsequent recipes, and Ability was the ease of finding and using those recipes.
